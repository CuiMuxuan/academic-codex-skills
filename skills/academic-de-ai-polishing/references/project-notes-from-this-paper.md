# Project Notes From This Paper

These notes distill what actually worked during a full-length review-paper polishing workflow for an English manuscript on humid multicomponent odorous-gas treatment. This is not a generic style note. It records which sections most often looked AI-shaped, what kinds of edits lowered that risk effectively, and in what order the work was safest to do.

Use this reference when:

- the manuscript is long and already substantially drafted
- the user wants to reduce obvious AI traces without destabilizing the structure
- section-by-section revision needs to be prioritized
- the draft includes many citations, figures, tables, and strong review-style synthesis

## Main Lesson

The most reliable way to lower AI trace in a long academic review is not to rewrite the whole paper into a new voice. The safer method is staged local intervention:

1. identify the visibly templated sections
2. rewrite only medium- and high-risk paragraphs
3. restore authorial judgment where the text sounds too smooth
4. fix figure/table lead-ins and synthesis sentences
5. re-check cadence after the structure is already stable

Whole-section regeneration is much riskier. It often weakens citation logic, homogenizes tone, and introduces a new layer of smooth but artificial prose.

## Sections That Most Often Looked AI-Shaped

### 1. Title and Abstract

Observed risk:

- the writing became too globally polished
- novelty statements sounded pre-packaged
- summary rhythm was elegant but impersonal

What worked:

- replacing generic review-summary language with the actual organizing lens of the paper
- narrowing claims instead of intensifying them
- making at least one sentence more concrete than rhetorically smooth

### 2. Introduction

Observed risk:

- "existing reviews are useful, but limited" structures became too regular
- critique sounded perfectly staged
- the novelty claim felt overly self-aware

What worked:

- grounding the motivation in a real process-selection problem
- making the limitation of prior reviews more operational and less theatrical
- reducing paired sentence symmetry

### 3. Technology Review Sections

Observed risk:

- repeated "mechanism -> strengths -> weaknesses" subsection rhythm
- too many balanced benefit-versus-limitation sentences
- headline removals were summarized without enough operating context

What worked:

- tying each subsection to a different evaluative pressure: mechanism, operating window, failure route, or integration role
- adding one explicit selection judgment where the literature was otherwise only being summarized
- qualifying metrics with loading, stability, humidity, or by-product consequences

### 4. Hybrid Design Section

Observed risk:

- this section carried the strongest AI signal
- route-by-route labeling encouraged checklist prose
- decision logic read like outline expansion

What worked:

- reframing route comparison as burden allocation rather than equipment listing
- making each route answer a different engineering question
- replacing numbered checklist logic with trade-off logic

### 5. Future Perspectives

Observed risk:

- "more research is needed" language accumulated quickly
- standardization appeals sounded generic

What worked:

- specifying what type of missing evidence mattered
- separating reporting gaps from scientific gaps
- replacing aspiration with priority and scope

## Paragraph Types That Needed Special Attention

### Figure Lead-In Paragraphs

Observed risk:

- figure mentions sounded inserted after writing
- pre-figure sentences often just announced the figure

What worked:

- rewriting lead-ins so the figure answered an argument already underway
- making the figure a consequence of the logic, not a detached visual supplement

### Post-Figure Takeaway Sentences

Observed risk:

- after a figure, the prose often dropped into bland recap

What worked:

- adding one short sentence that states what the figure changes in the interpretation
- making that sentence selective rather than descriptive

### Table-Linked Summary Sentences

Observed risk:

- tables encouraged compressed catalogue prose
- Ref.-heavy rows tempted the text to sound compiled rather than authored

What worked:

- using tables for storage and prose for judgment
- ensuring the text interprets the table rather than duplicating it

## Most Effective Revision Sequence

The following order proved safer than style polishing from page 1 to the end:

1. stabilize structure, headings, and citation placement
2. identify high-risk sections by reviewer impression
3. rewrite the worst paragraphs only
4. revise figure and table transitions
5. compress overlong citation strings without erasing evidence roles
6. smooth residual cadence only after the manuscript already sounds authored

This order matters. If cadence polishing happens too early, later structural edits often recreate the same AI-shaped patterns.

## What Successfully Lowered AI Trace

### Implicit transitions

Replacing explicit connectors with topic continuation worked better than merely deleting connectors. The prose improved when one sentence created the need for the next.

### Controlled asymmetry

The manuscript sounded more human once not every paragraph was equally balanced. A few sharper judgment sentences helped the surrounding prose feel authored.

### Evidence-bound criticism

The strongest reductions in AI feel came from sentences that weighed what the literature did not justify, not just what it reported.

### Section-specific rhetoric

Different sections needed different pressures:

- introduction: decision-usefulness
- review body: mechanism and operating boundaries
- hybrid section: burden allocation and trade-off
- future work: specificity and ranking of gaps

## What Did Not Work Well

- regenerating already acceptable paragraphs just for style uniformity
- over-compressing citations until interpretive nuance disappeared
- replacing all explicit transitions with abrupt jumps
- making the prose dry in the name of "removing AI voice"
- forcing every paragraph into a hyper-critical tone

The goal is not anti-style. The goal is authored style.

## Reliable Reviewer-Facing Assessment Framework

When estimating visible AI trace in a long manuscript, evaluate three separate questions:

1. would a human reader notice obvious templated phrasing?
2. would the paper feel under-edited or mass-generated?
3. are any specific sections still carrying disproportionate risk?

This works better than a single global judgment. In practice, a manuscript can improve substantially overall while still retaining a few locally high-risk paragraphs.

## Practical Markers Of Improvement

The manuscript is usually in a much safer state when:

- the introduction no longer sounds like a review-template preamble
- hybrid-design paragraphs sound like engineering reasoning rather than taxonomy
- future-work sentences specify missing evidence instead of requesting "more studies"
- figure/table transitions feel argument-driven
- no paragraph depends on connector stacking to move forward

## Transfer Rule

The lessons above generalize well to long English review papers in engineering, environmental science, materials, and related technical fields. They are less reliable for short experimental articles, humanities prose, or heavily narrative scholarship, where the rhythm and expectations differ.
